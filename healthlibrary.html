Cycle disorder

PMS & PMDD

Premenstrual syndrome and its severe form, PMDD. Why they happen, what the difference is, and what you can bring to your doctor.

Luteal phaseMoodHormonesSerotonin
What is it

PMS appears in the luteal phase, the two weeks before a period, and disappears once menstruation begins. PMDD is the severe form, classified as a distinct mood disorder since 2013. The distinction matters: PMS causes discomfort, while PMDD causes significant disruption to daily life, relationships, and work.

~50%experience PMS
3–8%experience PMDD
What causes it

The root is an abnormal brain sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations, particularly the drop in estrogen and progesterone at the end of the luteal phase. Research published in 2023 found that a key part of the serotonin system responds differently in people with PMDD compared to those without it. This is a neurobiological difference, not a personality trait or emotional weakness. Genetic factors and stress history also play a role.

Worth knowing
PMDD is not a matter of being "too sensitive." It is a condition rooted in how the brain processes hormonal signals. Recognising that distinction is often the first step toward getting the right support.
Symptoms
Mood swings, irritability, anger
Anxiety or feeling overwhelmed
Depression or hopelessness (PMDD)
Brain fog, difficulty concentrating
Bloating and water retention
Breast tenderness
Headaches or migraines
Fatigue and sleep disruption
Food cravings, especially carbs or sugar
Withdrawing from daily activities
What doctors look at

Doctors will generally want to understand the pattern and severity of your symptoms over time, which is why tracking them across at least two cycles is often the starting point. From there, approaches vary depending on your situation. Some focus on the hormonal cycle itself, others on how the brain responds to hormonal shifts. Lifestyle factors like sleep, movement, and nutrition are often part of the conversation too.

Questions to ask your doctor
"Based on my symptoms, do you think this is PMS or could it be PMDD?
"What would you want me to track before our next appointment, and for how long?
"Are there lifestyle changes that tend to help with the type of symptoms I'm describing?
"What are the main approaches available to me, and what are the differences between them?
"If my symptoms are affecting my relationships or work, does that change what we consider?
Important
If premenstrual symptoms are significantly affecting your daily life, or if you ever experience thoughts of self-harm, please speak with a doctor. This is a recognised condition with effective support available.

Hormonal disorder

PCOS

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome. One of the most common hormonal conditions, yet one of the most misunderstood.

AndrogensInsulin resistanceIrregular cyclesFertility
What is it

PCOS is a hormonal condition characterised by elevated androgens, irregular or absent ovulation, and often multiple underdeveloped follicles on the ovaries. Not everyone with PCOS has visible cysts, and the name is a little misleading because of this. Diagnosis is based on a combination of criteria, not on imaging alone.

1 in 10women worldwide
~70%go undiagnosed
What causes it

PCOS tends to involve a combination of genetic predisposition, insulin resistance, and low-grade inflammation. When insulin levels are elevated, the ovaries can respond by producing more androgens. This disrupts the development of follicles and makes regular ovulation harder. The result is a cascade of symptoms that can look very different from person to person.

Worth knowing
PCOS is not one condition with one presentation. Some women experience it mainly through metabolic symptoms, others through androgenic ones like acne or hair growth, and others primarily through irregular cycles. Your experience is valid even if it looks different from someone else's.
Symptoms
Irregular, infrequent, or absent periods
Excess hair growth on face, chest, or back
Acne that does not respond to usual approaches
Hair thinning at the crown
Weight gain around the midsection
Difficulty losing weight
Darkened skin patches
Difficulty conceiving
What doctors look at

Because PCOS presents so differently, doctors will usually look at the full picture before suggesting any path forward. Blood tests to check hormone levels and how your body handles blood sugar are common early steps. From there, the approach depends on which symptoms are most present. Women focused on cycle regulation, androgenic symptoms, metabolic health, or fertility may be guided toward quite different conversations. Many women find that movement and nutrition changes make a meaningful difference, and this is often explored early in care.

Long-term awareness
Untreated PCOS is associated with a higher risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular changes, and endometrial concerns over time. Regular check-ins with a doctor matter, even when symptoms feel manageable.
Questions to ask your doctor
"What type of PCOS do I have, and which symptoms should we prioritise?
"Should we test my insulin levels, and how would the results change the approach?
"What lifestyle changes would make the biggest difference for my specific situation?
"How does PCOS affect my fertility, and what should I know now even if I am not trying to conceive yet?
"What should I be monitoring long-term, and how often?

Reproductive condition

Endometriosis

A painful condition affecting 1 in 10 women. Tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus. On average, diagnosis takes 7 to 12 years.

Chronic painFertilityInflammationOften misdiagnosed
What is it

Endometrial-like tissue grows outside the uterus, on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, bladder, and pelvic lining. During menstruation, this tissue bleeds internally with nowhere to go. Over time this causes inflammation, scar tissue, and adhesions. Symptom severity does not reflect disease staging. Someone with mild endometriosis can be in severe pain, and someone with extensive disease may have few symptoms.

~10%of women affected
7–12 yrsaverage to diagnosis
~40%of infertile women
Why diagnosis takes so long

Period pain has been normalised for generations. Women are regularly told "it's just bad periods" and sent away. Definitive diagnosis requires a surgical procedure, which creates a high bar for investigation. Symptoms also overlap significantly with IBS, bladder conditions, and pelvic floor issues, leading to frequent misdiagnosis. Many women visit multiple doctors over years before anyone considers endometriosis.

Important
Painful periods that interfere with daily life are not normal. If pain is not adequately managed, or if it is getting worse over time, you deserve to be taken seriously. Advocate for yourself, and seek a second opinion if needed.
Symptoms
Severe, debilitating period pain
Chronic pelvic pain between periods
Pain during or after sex
Painful bowel movements or urination
Heavy periods with clots
Significant bloating around periods
Fatigue and low energy
Difficulty conceiving
What doctors look at

Doctors will look at your full symptom picture, cycle history, and quality of life impact. Because definitive diagnosis is surgical, many doctors first explore whether symptom management through hormonal options gives relief, which can also act as a diagnostic indicator. Surgical options exist for those where other approaches are insufficient. Pelvic physiotherapy and pain-focused support are increasingly recognised as important alongside other care. Working with a specialist who focuses specifically on endometriosis often makes a significant difference.

Questions to ask your doctor
"Could my symptoms be consistent with endometriosis, and what would investigating that involve?
"What are the non-surgical options for symptom management, and what are the trade-offs?
"If I wanted a definitive diagnosis, what would that process look like?
"Should I be seeing an endometriosis specialist rather than a general gynaecologist?
"How might this affect my fertility, and when should we be having that conversation?

Life transition

Perimenopause & Menopause

Not a disease. A major hormonal transition that deserves proper information, not dismissal. It often begins years earlier than most people expect.

EstrogenProgesteroneHRTBrain healthBone density
What is it

Perimenopause is the transitional phase, often beginning in the early to mid-40s and sometimes in the late 30s. It can last anywhere from four to ten years. During this time, hormone levels fluctuate erratically rather than declining in a smooth, predictable way. Menopause itself is defined as twelve consecutive months without a period, occurring at a median age of around 51 in Europe.

Often unrecognised
Perimenopause symptoms can appear years before any cycle changes. Anxiety, brain fog, sleep disruption, and joint pain are common early signs. Many women in their early 40s are told they are too young for menopause-related symptoms. If something feels off and is cyclical or new, it is worth exploring.
Symptoms
Hot flashes and night sweats
Irregular or changing periods
Sleep disturbances
Mood changes, anxiety, irritability
Brain fog and memory changes
Vaginal dryness
Reduced libido
Joint pain and stiffness
Hair thinning
Urinary changes and more frequent UTIs
What doctors look at

Doctors will look at your symptoms, age, cycle history, and overall health to understand where you are in the transition. Hormone testing can be helpful in some cases, though in perimenopause levels fluctuate so much that a single result is rarely the whole story. Conversations about managing symptoms often cover a range of options, from lifestyle and sleep support to hormonal therapies. The evidence around hormonal support has evolved significantly in recent years, and many doctors now have a more nuanced view of both the benefits and the risks than was common a decade ago.

Worth checking
Menopause symptoms overlap significantly with thyroid conditions. If you are in perimenopause, ask your doctor to also test thyroid function. These two things are frequently confused and both are worth ruling in or out.
Questions to ask your doctor
"Could my symptoms be related to perimenopause, even at my age?
"Should we also test my thyroid, given how much the symptoms can overlap?
"What options are available to me for managing symptoms, and what are the differences between them?
"How does my personal health history affect what would be appropriate for me?
"What should I be thinking about for bone and heart health as I move through this transition?

Hormonal / Endocrine

Thyroid Health & Women

A small butterfly-shaped gland with enormous influence on energy, mood, weight, cycles, and fertility. Women are 5 to 8 times more likely to develop thyroid disorders than men.

TSHHypothyroidismHashimoto'sFertilityCycle impact
What is it

The thyroid produces hormones that regulate metabolism, body temperature, heart rate, energy, and the reproductive system. When it is not functioning well, the effects reach almost every part of your body. Thyroid dysfunction directly impacts menstrual cycles, fertility, mood, and cognitive function, yet it is frequently missed because its symptoms look so much like other things.

5–8×more common in women
1 in 8women affected in lifetime
Understanding the conditions
ConditionWhat happensKey symptoms
Hypothyroidism
Underactive		 Too little hormone is produced and metabolism slows Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, depression, brain fog, heavy periods, hair loss, constipation
Hyperthyroidism
Overactive		 Too much hormone is produced and metabolism accelerates Weight loss, rapid heartbeat, anxiety, heat intolerance, lighter or absent periods, tremor, insomnia
Hashimoto's
Autoimmune		 The immune system attacks the thyroid over time Similar to hypothyroidism, can fluctuate in earlier stages. The most common thyroid condition overall.
Impact on cycles and fertility

An underactive thyroid tends to produce heavier, more frequent, or irregular periods, and can interfere with ovulation. An overactive thyroid tends to produce lighter or absent periods. Many women find that once the thyroid is properly supported, their cycles naturally settle and fertility improves. This connection is under-recognised, and thyroid testing is not always offered when cycles are the presenting concern.

Worth knowing
Thyroid symptoms look a lot like perimenopause, depression, chronic fatigue, and PCOS. A blood test can distinguish thyroid dysfunction. When you ask your doctor, request a full panel: TSH, Free T4, Free T3, and TPO antibodies. A TSH result alone does not always give the full picture.
What doctors look at

Diagnosis starts with blood tests, though interpreting the results involves nuance. What is considered a "normal" TSH range is debated among specialists, and some women feel symptoms at levels that fall within the standard reference range. Doctors will look at your full symptom picture alongside your results. Support for thyroid conditions is generally long-term and requires regular monitoring as needs can change over time.

Questions to ask your doctor
"Can we run a full thyroid panel, including Free T3, Free T4, and TPO antibodies, not just TSH?
"My results are within the normal range but I still have symptoms. Is there more to explore?
"Could my irregular cycles or fertility challenges be related to thyroid function?
"If I have Hashimoto's, what lifestyle factors are worth addressing alongside any treatment?
"How often should we recheck my levels, and what would prompt us to revisit the approach?

Contraception & Hormones

Hormonal Contraception

How different methods work, what they do to your cycle, and how they can affect mood, libido, and wellbeing. Honestly explained.

The pillIUDImplantMood effectsNatural cycles
How it works

Hormonal contraception introduces synthetic estrogen and/or progestins into the body, suppressing the natural hormonal cycle. Most methods prevent ovulation, thicken cervical mucus, and alter the uterine lining. One thing many women are not told: on combined hormonal methods the monthly bleed is a withdrawal bleed, not a true period driven by your own hormones. Your natural cycle is paused for as long as you use hormonal contraception.

Method overview
MethodHormonesKey points
Combined pillSynthetic estrogen and progestinSuppresses ovulation fully. Can improve certain symptoms for some. May affect mood, libido, or lubrication in others. Small blood clot risk.
Progestin-only pillProgestin onlyMay or may not suppress ovulation. Suitable for those who cannot take estrogen. Irregular spotting is common in early months.
Hormonal IUDProgestin released locallyVery low systemic hormone levels. Many stop having periods. Often reported to have a lower mood impact. Lasts 3 to 8 years depending on type.
ImplantProgestinA small rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm. Suppresses ovulation in most. Irregular bleeding common early on. Lasts 3 years.
Patch and vaginal ringEstrogen and progestinSimilar hormonal profile to the combined pill, delivered through the skin or vaginally rather than orally.
Copper IUDNo hormonesWorks through the effect of copper on sperm. Natural cycle is fully preserved. Can cause heavier or more painful periods. Lasts 5 to 10 years.
Mood and wellbeing

A large study involving over one million women found associations between hormonal contraception and increased rates of depression and antidepressant use, particularly in younger women and with certain formulations. At the same time, many women report feeling better on hormonal contraception because it reduces painful or disruptive symptoms. The effect is genuinely individual, and neither experience is more valid than the other.

Worth knowing
If you notice changes to your mood, libido, or energy after starting a method, that is important information. Trying a different formulation, or a different method type, often changes the picture significantly. You do not have to stay with something that does not feel right.
Interactions with other conditions

Hormonal contraception intersects with several other conditions in ways worth knowing about. For endometriosis and PMDD, certain formulations are used as part of symptom management, though the type of progestin matters and not all are equally suited to everyone. For PCOS, hormonal contraception can help with cycle regularity and androgenic symptoms but does not address the underlying metabolic picture. For those on thyroid support, oral estrogen-containing methods can affect how thyroid levels are interpreted in blood tests, so your doctor should know if you start or stop these.

Coming off hormonal contraception

Natural cycles typically resume within one to six months, though this varies by method and by person. Some women experience a period of adjustment involving irregular cycles, acne, or mood changes while the body rebalances. If periods do not return within three months, it is worth speaking with a doctor.

Important
There is no single best method. The right choice depends on your health history, your symptoms, your fertility intentions, and how your body responds. This is here to help you walk into that conversation better informed.
Questions to ask your doctor
"Given my health history and what I want from contraception, what would you suggest and why?
"I have noticed changes to my mood or libido since starting. Is that something we should address by changing method or formulation?
"I have endometriosis, PMDD, or PCOS. How does that change what would be most appropriate for me?
"What should I expect when I eventually come off hormonal contraception?

Uterine condition

Adenomyosis

Often called endometriosis's lesser-known sister. Tissue grows into the muscle wall of the uterus itself, causing heavy bleeding and chronic pelvic pain.

Heavy periodsPelvic painEnlarged uterusOften misdiagnosed
What is it

Endometrial tissue grows into the myometrium, the muscular wall of the uterus, rather than outside it. During menstruation, this tissue bleeds internally, causing the uterus to enlarge and cramping to become more intense. Adenomyosis is estimated to affect 20 to 35% of women and frequently coexists with endometriosis or fibroids. Improvements in MRI imaging have made diagnosis more accessible than it once was.

20–35%estimated prevalence
Symptoms
Heavy, prolonged periods
Severe menstrual cramps
Chronic pelvic pressure or fullness
Enlarged and tender uterus
Pain during sex
Significant bloating before or during periods
What doctors look at

Doctors will assess your symptoms, pelvic exam findings, and may refer for an MRI or specialist ultrasound. Because adenomyosis often coexists with other conditions, the full picture matters before deciding on a path. Hormonal approaches that suppress the cycle are often explored to assess whether they bring relief. Newer non-surgical options have expanded in recent years. Surgical options exist for more severe cases. The right path depends on the severity of your symptoms, your fertility plans, and how the condition is affecting your daily life.

Worth knowing
Adenomyosis frequently occurs alongside endometriosis and fibroids. If you have been investigated for one, it may be worth asking whether the others have been considered.
Questions to ask your doctor
"Could adenomyosis explain my symptoms, and what investigation would help clarify that?
"Should we also look at whether endometriosis or fibroids are present at the same time?
"What non-surgical options are available, and how long would it take to know if they are working?
"How might this affect my fertility, and when should we be discussing that?

Uterine condition

Uterine Fibroids

Non-cancerous growths that up to 80% of women will develop by age 50. Rarely discussed, yet extremely common.

Non-cancerousHeavy bleedingPressure symptomsFertility impact
What is it

Fibroids are benign growths that develop in or on the uterus. They are stimulated by estrogen and progesterone and typically shrink after menopause. They range from microscopic to very large and can grow singly or in clusters. Black women are 2 to 3 times more likely to develop fibroids, tend to develop them earlier, and experience more severe symptoms. This disparity remains significantly under-researched.

up to 80%of women by age 50
2–3×higher risk in Black women
Worth knowing
Many fibroids cause no symptoms and are discovered incidentally during routine scans. Finding one does not automatically mean treatment is needed. Whether to pursue any action depends entirely on whether symptoms are present and how much they affect daily life.
Symptoms
Heavy or prolonged periods
Pelvic pressure or fullness
Frequent or urgent urination
Constipation or lower back pain
Painful periods
Difficulty conceiving (depends on location)
What doctors look at

The approach to fibroids depends entirely on your symptoms, the size and location of the fibroids, your age, and your fertility plans. For asymptomatic fibroids, watchful waiting with regular monitoring is often entirely appropriate. When symptoms are present, conversations typically move through a range from hormonal management to minimally invasive procedures to surgery. Fibroids generally shrink naturally after menopause, which is a relevant consideration in timing decisions for women approaching that transition.

Questions to ask your doctor
"Where are my fibroids located, and how does that affect what symptoms I might expect?
"Given my symptoms and my stage of life, what would you recommend as a starting point?
"What are the minimally invasive options available, and am I a candidate for them?
"If I want to preserve my fertility, how does that change what we consider?
"If we take a watchful waiting approach, what would prompt us to act sooner?
