Cycle disorder

PMS & PMDD

Premenstrual syndrome and its severe form, PMDD — why they happen, what the difference is, and what actually helps.

Luteal phase Mood Hormones Serotonin
What is it

PMS appears in the luteal phase — the two weeks before a period — and disappears once menstruation begins. PMDD (Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder) is the severe form, classified as a distinct mood disorder in DSM-5 since 2013. The distinction matters: PMS causes discomfort; PMDD causes significant functional impairment.

~50%experience PMS
3–8%experience PMDD
What causes it

The root is abnormal brain sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations — particularly the estrogen and progesterone drop at the end of the luteal phase. The serotonin system responds differently in those affected. A 2023 Max Planck Institute study found that serotonin transporter density increases from the ovulatory to the premenstrual phase in PMDD patients — the opposite of unaffected women. Genetic factors, stress history, and HPA axis reactivity also play a significant role.

Key insight
"PMDD is not a personality flaw or a matter of being 'too sensitive.' It is a neurobiological condition rooted in how the brain responds to hormones."
Symptoms
Mood swings, irritability, anger
Anxiety or feeling overwhelmed
Depression or hopelessness (PMDD)
Brain fog, difficulty concentrating
Bloating and water retention
Breast tenderness
Headaches or migraines
Fatigue and sleep disruption
Food cravings (carbs, sugar)
Reduced interest in daily activities
Treatment options
💊
SSRIs — gold standard for PMDD
Fluoxetine and sertraline have the strongest evidence. Can be taken continuously or only during the luteal phase. Uniquely, effects appear within hours to days — not the weeks typical for depression treatment.
🔵
Combined oral contraceptives
Drospirenone-containing pills (e.g. Yaz) show evidence specifically for reducing PMDD. Some formulations can worsen mood — the progestin type matters.
🏃
Aerobic exercise
Strong evidence for improving mood, energy, and physical symptoms. Royal College of Obstetricians recommends as first-line alongside CBT and vitamin B6.
🥗
Dietary adjustments
Reduce sugar, caffeine, alcohol, and salt in the luteal phase. Calcium 1,200mg/day + vitamin D associated with symptom reduction. Magnesium may help bloating and mood.
🧠
CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)
Strong evidence as a complementary approach. Helps modify thought patterns and responses to premenstrual symptoms.
Important
If premenstrual symptoms significantly impact relationships, work, or daily functioning — especially if you experience thoughts of self-harm — please speak with a doctor. PMDD is a recognised medical condition with effective treatments.

Hormonal disorder

PCOS

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome — one of the most common hormonal conditions, yet one of the most misunderstood.

Androgens Insulin resistance Irregular cycles Fertility
What is it

A hormonal disorder characterised by elevated androgens, irregular or absent ovulation, and often multiple follicles on the ovaries. Not all people with PCOS have visible cysts. Diagnosis is based on a combination of criteria — not imaging alone (Rotterdam criteria: 2 of 3 required).

1 in 10women worldwide
~70%go undiagnosed
What causes it

A combination of genetic predisposition, insulin resistance, and chronic low-grade inflammation. High insulin signals the ovaries to produce more testosterone, which disrupts follicle development and ovulation. Elevated androgens then drive symptoms like acne, excess hair growth, and hair loss.

Key insight
PCOS is not one condition — it presents very differently. Some have primarily metabolic symptoms, others androgenic, others mainly reproductive. Treatment should be tailored to the individual's symptom profile.
Symptoms
Irregular, infrequent, or absent periods
Excess hair growth (face, chest, back)
Acne not responding to treatment
Hair thinning at the crown
Weight gain around the midsection
Difficulty losing weight
Darkened skin patches (acanthosis nigricans)
Difficulty conceiving
Treatment options
🥦
Lifestyle — first-line treatment
Even 5–10% body weight reduction can significantly improve hormones and restore ovulation. Low-glycaemic diet reduces insulin spikes. Strength training particularly effective for improving insulin sensitivity.
💊
Metformin
Reduces insulin resistance. Often improves cycle regularity, reduces androgen levels, and may support weight management.
🔵
Combined oral contraceptives
Regulates cycles and reduces androgenic symptoms (acne, hair growth). Not a cure — does not treat the underlying metabolic cause.
🌿
Inositol (especially myo-inositol)
Growing body of evidence for improving insulin sensitivity and ovarian function. Generally well-tolerated with few side effects.
Long-term considerations
Untreated PCOS increases risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and endometrial cancer. Regular monitoring of metabolic markers is important.

Reproductive condition

Endometriosis

A painful condition affecting 1 in 10 women — tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus. Diagnosis takes 7–12 years on average.

Chronic pain Fertility Inflammation Often misdiagnosed
What is it

Endometrial-like tissue grows outside the uterus — on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, bladder, and pelvic lining. During menstruation, this tissue bleeds internally but has nowhere to go, causing inflammation, scar tissue (adhesions), and chronic pain. Symptom severity does not correlate with disease staging.

~10%of women affected
7–12 yrsaverage to diagnosis
~40%of infertile women
Why diagnosis takes so long

Period pain has been normalised for generations — women are often told "just bad periods." Definitive diagnosis requires laparoscopy (surgery). The overlap with IBS, bladder conditions, and pelvic floor dysfunction leads to frequent misdiagnosis. Many women are dismissed by multiple doctors before diagnosis.

Important
Painful periods that interfere with daily life are not normal. If painkillers don't adequately control period pain, or if pain is getting worse over time — advocate for yourself with a doctor or seek a specialist.
Symptoms
Severe debilitating period pain
Chronic pelvic pain (non-cyclical)
Pain during or after sex
Painful bowel movements or urination
Heavy periods with clots
Bloating ("endo belly")
Fatigue and low energy
Difficulty conceiving
Treatment options
💊
Hormonal therapies
Combined pill, progestins, GnRH agonists suppress the hormonal cycle that drives lesion growth. Do not remove existing endometriosis but manage symptoms effectively for many.
🔬
Laparoscopic excision surgery — gold standard
Excision (cutting out) is preferred over ablation (burning). Should be performed by an endometriosis specialist. Evidence shows specialist centres have significantly better outcomes.
🐟
Anti-inflammatory lifestyle
Omega-3 rich diet, reducing processed foods and alcohol. Limited clinical evidence but consistently reported as helpful by patients and supported by mechanistic reasoning.
🤲
Pelvic physiotherapy & pain psychology
Addresses secondary muscular dysfunction and central sensitisation. Increasingly recognised as an important part of a multimodal treatment approach.

Life transition

Perimenopause & Menopause

Not a disease — a major hormonal transition that deserves proper information, not dismissal. Often begins years earlier than most expect.

Estrogen Progesterone HRT Brain health Bone density
What is it

Perimenopause is the transitional period — often beginning in the early to mid-40s, sometimes late 30s — and can last 4–10 years. During this time, hormone levels fluctuate erratically rather than declining smoothly. Menopause is defined as 12 consecutive months without a period, occurring at a median age of approximately 51 in Europe.

Often unrecognised
Perimenopause symptoms can appear years before any cycle changes — anxiety, brain fog, sleep disruption, and joint pain are common early signs. Many women in their early 40s are told they are "too young" for menopause-related symptoms.
Symptoms
Hot flashes and night sweats
Irregular or changing periods
Sleep disturbances
Mood changes, anxiety, irritability
Brain fog and memory changes
Vaginal dryness
Reduced libido
Joint pain and stiffness
Hair thinning
Urinary changes and increased UTIs
HRT — current evidence (2024–2025)

Scientific consensus has shifted significantly since the 2002 Women's Health Initiative study, which overstated breast cancer risk due to study design issues. The 2022 Menopause Society guidelines and 2025 IMS World Congress confirm: for women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause onset, benefits typically outweigh risks for most women. The portion of the original WHI study showing breast cancer risk has been substantially revised.

✅
Current consensus — generally safe for most women under 60
No increased initial breast cancer risk. After 5+ years of combined HRT, approximately 0.4% increased absolute risk — comparable to lifestyle factors like alcohol consumption.
🔄
Types and delivery routes
Estrogen + progesterone (for those with a uterus); estrogen-only (after hysterectomy). Available as pills, patches, gels, and sprays. Transdermal (patches, gels) has a significantly lower blood clot risk than oral forms.
🦴
Additional benefits
Protects bone density (reduces osteoporosis risk). May reduce cardiovascular risk when started early in the menopausal transition. Growing evidence for protecting cognitive function and brain health.
Worth knowing
Menopause symptoms overlap significantly with thyroid disorders. If you are in perimenopause, ask your doctor to test thyroid function — only 1 in 4 women discussing menopause symptoms are also tested for thyroid disease.

Hormonal / Endocrine

Thyroid Health & Women

A small butterfly-shaped gland with enormous influence on energy, mood, weight, cycles, and fertility. Women are 5–8× more likely to develop thyroid disorders than men.

TSH Hypothyroidism Hashimoto's Fertility Cycle impact
What is it

The thyroid gland produces T3 and T4 hormones that regulate metabolism, body temperature, heart rate, energy, and the reproductive system. Thyroid dysfunction directly impacts menstrual cycles, fertility, pregnancy outcomes, and mood — yet is frequently missed or conflated with other conditions.

5–8×more common in women
1 in 8women affected in lifetime
Condition comparison
Condition What happens Key symptoms
Hypothyroidism
Underactive		 Too little hormone produced; metabolism slows Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, depression, brain fog, heavy periods, hair loss, constipation
Hyperthyroidism
Overactive		 Too much hormone produced; metabolism accelerates Weight loss, rapid heartbeat, anxiety, heat intolerance, lighter or absent periods, tremor, insomnia
Hashimoto's
Autoimmune		 Immune system attacks the thyroid gland Same as hypothyroidism; can fluctuate early on. Diagnosed by elevated TPO antibodies. Most common thyroid disorder.
Impact on cycles & fertility

Hypothyroidism → heavy, irregular, or more frequent periods; anovulation; worsened PMS. Hyperthyroidism → lighter, less frequent, or absent periods. Treating the underlying thyroid condition often normalises menstrual cycles and significantly improves fertility outcomes.

Key insight
Thyroid symptoms overlap heavily with perimenopause, depression, chronic fatigue, and PCOS. A TSH blood test can distinguish thyroid dysfunction. Ask specifically for a full thyroid panel: TSH, Free T4, Free T3, and TPO antibodies.
Treatment options
💊
Levothyroxine (hypothyroidism)
Synthetic T4, taken daily — usually for life. Most people feel significantly better within weeks of reaching optimal dose. Dose requires regular TSH monitoring.
🌿
Hashimoto's — levothyroxine + lifestyle
Levothyroxine treats the resulting hypothyroidism. Anti-inflammatory lifestyle, selenium, vitamin D optimisation, and potentially gluten-free diet may help reduce antibody levels and inflammation.
🏥
Hyperthyroidism / Graves' disease
Antithyroid medications (carbimazole, propylthiouracil), radioactive iodine therapy, or surgery. Managed by an endocrinologist.

Contraception & Hormones

Hormonal Contraception

How different methods work hormonally, what they do to your cycle, and how they can affect mood, libido, and wellbeing — honestly explained.

The pill IUD Implant Mood effects Natural cycles
How it works

All hormonal contraceptives introduce synthetic estrogen and/or progestins, suppressing the natural hormonal cycle. They prevent ovulation, thicken cervical mucus, and thin the uterine lining. On combined pills, the "period" is a withdrawal bleed — not a true menstrual period driven by your own hormones.

Method comparison
Method Hormones Key effects & considerations
Combined pill (COC) Synthetic estrogen + progestin Fully suppresses ovulation. Can improve acne and PMS in some. May affect mood, libido, or lubrication in others. Slight blood clot risk.
Progestin-only pill Progestin only May or may not suppress ovulation. Thickens cervical mucus. Suitable if estrogen is contraindicated. Irregular spotting common initially.
Hormonal IUD
e.g. Mirena		 Levonorgestrel (local progestin) Acts locally — very low systemic absorption. Many stop having periods. Lower mood impact reported by many users. Lasts 3–8 years.
Implant
e.g. Nexplanon		 Etonogestrel (progestin) Inserted under skin of upper arm. Suppresses ovulation in most. Irregular bleeding common early on. Lasts 3 years. Some report mood changes.
Patch & vaginal ring Estrogen + progestin Similar hormonal profile to combined pill. Different delivery route may suit some better.
Copper IUD
Non-hormonal		 No hormones Works via copper ions toxic to sperm. Natural cycle fully preserved. Can cause heavier or more painful periods. Lasts 5–10 years.
Mood & wellbeing

A large Danish registry study of over 1 million women found associations between hormonal contraception and increased risk of depression diagnosis and antidepressant use — particularly in adolescents and with certain progestin types. However, many women report improved mood due to reduced PMS or pain. The effect is highly individual.

Key insight
Some feel better on hormonal contraception; others notice negative changes to mood, libido, or energy. Both experiences are valid. Trying a different formulation — or a different method entirely — often helps significantly.
Interactions with other conditions
Endometriosis: Combined pill and progestins commonly used to manage symptoms. Not a cure but often effective at controlling pain.
PCOS: Combined pill is first-line for androgenic symptoms and cycle regulation. Does not address the underlying metabolic cause.
PMDD: Drospirenone-containing pills (e.g. Yaz) show the best evidence. Some progestins can worsen mood in PMDD.
Thyroid: Oral estrogen-containing contraceptives can increase thyroid-binding globulin levels, affecting interpretation of thyroid tests. Inform your doctor if starting hormonal contraception while on thyroid medication.
Coming off hormonal contraception

Natural cycles can take 1–6 months to resume after stopping (longer after injectables). Some experience "post-pill syndrome" — irregular cycles, acne, or mood changes as the body rebalances. If natural cycles do not return within 3 months, speak with a doctor to rule out PCOS or other causes.

Important
There is no single 'best' contraceptive method. The right choice depends on personal health history, lifestyle, cycle goals, and individual response. This information is here to help you have an informed conversation with your healthcare provider.

Uterine condition

Adenomyosis

Often called "endometriosis's lesser-known sister" — tissue grows into the muscle wall of the uterus itself, causing heavy bleeding and chronic pelvic pain.

Heavy periods Pelvic pain Enlarged uterus Often misdiagnosed
What is it

Endometrial tissue grows into the myometrium — the muscular wall of the uterus — rather than outside it. This causes the uterus to enlarge and bleed internally during menstruation. Adenomyosis is estimated to affect 20–35% of women. It can be diffuse (spread throughout the wall) or focal (in one area). Confirmed via MRI or histology; ultrasound can suggest but not definitively diagnose it.

20–35%estimated prevalence
Key insight
Adenomyosis frequently coexists with endometriosis and fibroids. It has historically been under-recognised because definitive diagnosis previously required hysterectomy — improvements in MRI imaging have changed this.
Symptoms
Heavy, prolonged periods
Severe menstrual cramps
Chronic pelvic pressure or fullness
Enlarged and tender uterus
Pain during sex
Bloating before and during period
Treatment options
🔵
Hormonal IUD (Mirena) — often highly effective
Levonorgestrel delivered locally suppresses the adenomyotic tissue. Many patients experience dramatic reduction in bleeding and pain. Strong first-line option.
💊
Hormonal therapies
Progestins, GnRH agonists, or combined pill can suppress symptoms. GnRH agonists may be used short-term before surgery to reduce uterine size.
🏥
Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE)
A minimally invasive procedure blocking blood supply to the adenomyotic tissue. Growing evidence as a less invasive alternative to surgery.
⚕️
Hysterectomy — only definitive cure
Reserved for severe cases where other treatments have failed and the person does not wish to preserve fertility. Laparoscopic approach preferred where possible.

Uterine condition

Uterine Fibroids

Non-cancerous growths — up to 80% of women will develop them by age 50, yet they are rarely discussed openly.

Non-cancerous Heavy bleeding Pressure symptoms Fertility impact
What is it

Benign tumours (myomas or leiomyomas) growing in or on the uterus. They are stimulated by estrogen and progesterone and typically shrink after menopause. They can range from microscopic to the size of a grapefruit. Black women are 2–3× more likely to develop fibroids and tend to experience more severe symptoms at a younger age — a disparity that remains under-researched.

up to 80%of women by age 50
2–3×higher risk in Black women
Worth knowing
Many fibroids cause no symptoms at all and are discovered incidentally during routine scans. Treatment depends entirely on whether symptoms are present and their impact on quality of life — watchful waiting is a legitimate option for asymptomatic fibroids.
Symptoms
Heavy or prolonged periods
Pelvic pressure or fullness
Frequent or urgent urination
Constipation or lower back pain
Painful periods
Difficulty conceiving (location-dependent)
Treatment pathway

Treatment depends on fibroid size and location, symptom severity, and fertility plans.

👁
Watchful waiting (asymptomatic)
Appropriate for fibroids causing no symptoms. Regular monitoring via ultrasound. Many remain stable or shrink at menopause.
💊
Medication
GnRH agonists reduce fibroid size (temporary). Progestins or hormonal IUD can reduce bleeding. Newer oral GnRH antagonists (e.g. relugolix) offer effective symptom management without injection.
🏥
UAE or focused ultrasound
Uterine Artery Embolisation and MRI-guided focused ultrasound are minimally invasive options that shrink fibroids and preserve the uterus.
🔬
Myomectomy
Surgical removal of fibroids while preserving the uterus. Preferred for women wishing to conceive. Can be laparoscopic or open depending on size and location.
⚕️
Hysterectomy — definitive cure
Removes fibroids permanently. Reserved for severe cases where other treatments have failed and fertility preservation is not a priority.
